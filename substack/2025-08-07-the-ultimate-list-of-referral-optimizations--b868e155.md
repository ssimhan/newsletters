## Preconditions for referrals

### Get clear on definitions

If you aren‚Äôt clear on definitions, you and your entire team will go nuts.

Please let me save you the headache:

*   **Referrer**: The person making a referral.
    
*   **Referee**: The person being referred.
    
*   **Referral**: What the referrer gives the referee.
    

Next, let‚Äôs clarify channels:

*   **Referral** is when a customer recommends your product in a way that is measured and tracked by your product.
    
*   **Word of Mouth** is when this happens in an _untracked_ way.
    
*   Do not conflate referrals with **Add to Team**, which is about growing _within_ a logo. I‚Äôll write a separate essay on Add to Team.
    

### Ensure referrals make sense for your business

Referrals make sense:

*   For B2C companies acquiring more customers.
    
*   For B2B companies landing new logos.
    

Referrals are a good fit when:

*   Your product is not so expensive that it would be weird to recommend to a friend.
    
    *   Imagine the friction of referring a product that will cost the referee >$50k per year.
        
    *   Rule of thumb: under $500 a year is the sweet spot for B2C, and under $5k for B2B.
        
*   It is easy for users to identify who _specifically_ would be a good person to refer.
    
    *   In B2C, this is mostly a question of customers identifying ‚Äúwho do I know who is like me?‚Äù
        
    *   In B2B, this involves your customer identifying both _which company_ needs your solution and _which individual_ to contact.
        
        *   Easier in product-led B2B, where your users can just think about other users like themselves.
            
        *   Harder in sales-led B2B, where your users might not know the right person to contact.
            

### Ensure you are ready to invest in referrals

Typical conditions:

*   Product love: high [product-market fit](https://review.firstround.com/how-superhuman-built-an-engine-to-find-product-market-fit/), high [retention](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/what-is-good-retention-issue-29), high [net promoter score](https://blog.hubspot.com/service/what-is-a-good-net-promoter-score).
    
*   You _already_ see word of mouth: customers share your product organically.
    

It might be too early to invest in referrals if you don‚Äôt see these signs.

Work on product instead. Boost customer love.

## The Ultimate Referral Playbook

### Step 1: Get referral basics in place

The first step is to create low-friction ways for customers to refer.

Customers already _want_ to recommend your product.

So create pathways to create a behavior loop.

1.  Give users a referral link that they can share anywhere they want.
    
    1.  Copy link to clipboard in one click or tap.
        
    2.  Optionally include some persuasive text when the link is copied (_‚ÄúI think you'll like Kondo. Here's a link for 1 month free [https://app.trykondo.com/referral/2FB3V1](https://app.trykondo.com/referral/2FB3V1)_‚Äù).
        
2.  Create clear referral entry-points throughout the app.
    
    1.  Make entry-points **extremely** visible from the app home screen. Making it visually indistinguishable drops engagement 3x. Burying it beneath a click drops engagement 10x.
        
    2.  Use words (‚ÄúRefer‚Äù) to make the action crystal clear.
        
    3.  Use iconography (‚ÄúüéÅ‚Äù) to create visual identity.
        
    4.  If appropriate, pick a color for further visual identity.
        
    5.  Add a keyboard-driven method accessible anywhere in the app. For example, if your app has a Command Palette, implement **Cmd+K ‚Üí Refer**.
        
    6.  Support all platforms: Desktop, mobile, web admin, and any others.
        
3.  Instrument and optimize everything_:_
    
    1.  When existing customers engage with the new referral entry points.
        
    2.  When the referral link is copied to clipboard.
        
    3.  When the referral link is clicked by referees.
        
    4.  When a signup converts via the referral link.
        
    5.  How well those customers retain.
        

For now, we are _not remotely_ concerned with adding incentives, financial or otherwise. We are simply tapping into existing motivation.

But wait. Isn‚Äôt it obnoxious for customers to refer if there‚Äôs no incentive?

Not if they love your product so much that they _want_ to share it with friends. If you really have found product-market fit, your customers will _already_ organically refer other customers.

You might well have some storytelling around why customers might want to refer in the absence of an incentive:

*   Perhaps you‚Äôre running a [waitlist](https://substack.gauravvohra.com/p/the-waitlist-delusion) in closed beta, and you‚Äôre making it so that referrers can help referees jump the queue.
    
*   Perhaps your product‚Äôs value increases when there are more connections between users, like in consumer social. In this case, giving customers the ability to refer directly benefits them.
    

### Step 2: Layer referral incentives on top

With the basics in place, we turn to incentives.

The critical thing to remember is that incentives are primarily **an excuse to communicate with your customers about the ability to refer**.

The details of the incentive also matter. But not as much as the excuse to message them about it.

1.  Create a two-sided incentive.
    
    1.  Both the referrer and the referee receive something in return for a successful referral.
        
    2.  Unless you have an extremely strong reason otherwise, make the incentive symmetric. If nothing else, it‚Äôs easier to express in copy: ‚ÄúYou and your friend both get XYZ‚Äù.
        
2.  Make the incentive conditional on a _successful_ referral, not just _making_ the referral.
    
    1.  The referee must get deep enough into the user journey to be considered high-quality growth.
        
    2.  Usually, this is the point where the referee would pay, or when they have reached sufficient product usage depth.
        
    3.  For example, [Wispr Flow](http://wisprflow.ai/r/GAURAV39) unlocks the symmetric referral bonus when the referee either pays _or_ hits 2,000 words (which is the primary threshold to shift from free ‚Üí paid).
        
3.  Express the incentive as cash.
    
    1.  In SaaS, you might be used to seeing: ‚ÄúYou and your friend both get a free month‚Äù. But humans typically respond more favorably to cash: ‚ÄúYou and your friends both get $20‚Äù. Cash feels instant and concrete. A free month requires context.
        
4.  Don‚Äôt just express a free month incentive as cash; actually _make it_ cash.
    
    > When it comes to incentivizing customer referral programs, there are two options: offer discounts on your products or offer cash (or an equivalent). In most cases, cash is king.  
    >   
    > _‚Äî [Cash is king for customer referrals, Tremendous](https://www.tremendous.com/blog/cash-is-king-for-customer-referrals-5-examples/#case-no-1-it-s-an-infrequent-purchase)_
    
    1.  Use a vendor like [Tremendous](http://tremendous.com) to abstract away logistics.
        
5.  Update the journey to remind referrers and referees of the incentive.
    
    1.  Referrers should see mention of the incentive at all points in the journey of referring. Every bit of explanatory microcopy should remind them.
        
    2.  Referees should be notified of the incentive in all messages, landing pages, and sign-up screens.
        
6.  Notify referrers and referees when their incentives kick in.
    
    1.  Thank referrers when they successfully refer _and_ when the referee successfully joins. In these messages, remind them about the incentive.
        
    2.  Notify referees when their reward is unlocked. In this message, prompt them to start referring.
        
7.  Create incentive depth. Remember, incentives are primarily about _reasons to message_, so it‚Äôs a good thing to keep changing them.
    
    1.  Add more incentive as the referee gets deeper into the product.
        
        1.  You might give both sides $10 when the referee starts a subscription, and _another_ $10 when they pass a usage threshold.
            
        2.  This is especially powerful if the referrer has some ability to influence the referee‚Äôs usage.
            
    2.  Increase the incentive as referrers refer more to drive continued activity.
        
    3.  Layer non-monetary incentives like swag giveaways, invites to exclusive events, and public recognition.
        

An incentive will inevitably add volume to the top of your funnel.

However, it will also cause average referral quality to decline. This will show up in conversion and retention.

Unless you‚Äôve really messed something up, the former should outweigh the latter. So don‚Äôt be alarmed when you see referral to customer conversion tank, and month 1 customer churn spike.

Keep calm and measure incremental impact on the number of new customers retained past month 2.

### Step 3: Market the heck out of your referral program

Your referral program lives and dies by how many of your customers know about it.

So make sure your entire customer base is made aware.

1.  Launch the referral program to the entire customer base.
    
    1.  Notify customers in-app, via notifications, over email, and any other communication channel available.
        
    2.  Allow them to opt out of this type of communication without harming their ability to receive other messages, of course.
        
2.  Routinely run referral campaigns with your entire customer base.
    
    1.  I find the sweet spot is 2-4 times a year. Much more and customers might experience message fatigue and run out of people to refer.
        
    2.  Connect promotions with meaningful customer moments. For example, at Superhuman, we ran referral promotions during [Inbox Zero Week](https://blog.superhuman.com/inboxzeroweek/) and in the first week of the year to tap into the ‚Äúnew year, new me‚Äù feeling.
        
    3.  Use a deadline to create urgency.
        
3.  Front-load the ask at the beginning of the new user journey.
    
    1.  Create an [interruptive](https://substack.gauravvohra.com/i/161988452/interruptive) full-screen ask that tells all new customers about the ability to refer, and the incentive.
        
    2.  Founders often balk at asking new customers to refer so early in their journey. But when an incentive is at stake, many customers are very happy to hear about the offer¬†‚Äî especially in B2C. Everyone else will ignore it without further thought.
        
4.  Prompt users to refer at moments of peak happiness.
    
    1.  This makes the most sense when they‚Äôve completed some action that demonstrates product _love_. Think reaching a streak milestone in Duolingo, or giving a 5\* Airbnb rating.
        
5.  Mention the ability to refer throughout lifecycle messaging.
    
    1.  Advertise the ability to refer as a ‚Äúps‚Äù or ‚Äúby the way‚Äù in your activation emails.
        
    2.  Include a _dedicated_ email in your activation sequence about the ability to refer so that all new customers are made aware.
        
    3.  Make mentioning referrals a standard part of your new product announcement messages.
        
    4.  Create an ‚Äòinsiders‚Äô group of your top referrers with ongoing fun and community-oriented messaging.
        

### Step 4: Make your app a referral machine

You now have a well-structured and well-advertised referral mechanism.

Next, turn your app into a referral _machine_.

1.  Don‚Äôt just rely on users sharing a link. Create the ability for your app to _directly_ _message_ referees on behalf of referrers.
    
    1.  Pre-write the message to make it easier for users to hit ‚Äúsend‚Äù. Let referrers tweak it if they desire.
        
    2.  Make this message look like [a real message from the referrer](https://www.useronboard.com/how-mailbox-onboards-new-users/?slide=4), not a generic-looking message from a company.
        
    3.  Include information that goes beyond ‚ÄúI like this tool‚Äù. For example, include usage statistics that show the referrer is getting value.
        
2.  If possible, suggest specific referees. Make this as simple as tapping a few names and faces. Of course, this requires that your app has access to a user‚Äôs contacts.
    
    1.  Add a way for users to import contacts and provide a strong reason to do so. Maybe the product functions better with it, or maybe the referral incentive is reason enough!
        
    2.  Sort the list to put the ‚Äòbest‚Äô recommendations at the top. Use whatever signal you might have. At [Superhuman](http://superhuman.com), this is easy: we look at who the customer emails a lot. Other techniques include cross-referencing the contact list with your ‚Äúsigned up, but hasn‚Äôt paid‚Äù list, or prioritizing a pre-defined list of logos.
        
    3.  If you‚Äôre extremely confident in the recommendations, add a ‚Äúselect all‚Äù checkbox, and pre-select it, removing all friction!
        
3.  Suggest referees in targeted and specific contexts.
    
    1.  For example, [Kondo](https://app.trykondo.com/referral/2FB3V1) has a ‚Äúrefer‚Äù button in the sidebar when you look at a particular user‚Äôs profile, _and_ in the conversation with that user.

### Step 5: Combine referrals with other channels: casual contact, social, community, influencer, affiliate, and add to team

At the heart of every great growth strategy is [a collection of mutually reinforcing channels](https://substack.gauravvohra.com/i/152206659/amplification-effects).

Referrals are often one of them.

1.  Combine referrals with casual contact loops. A casual contact loop is any time a user using your product results in someone new seeing it. Think sharing a Loom, Figma, or DocuSign.
    
    1.  Embed your user‚Äôs referral link into any content they might share using your app.
        
        1.  For example, ‚ÄúSent via Superhuman‚Äù email signature [is each user‚Äôs referral link](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/elenaverna_growth-referralmarketing-plg-activity-7070442905269592065-1R9p/), letting them emit referral links and earn incentives _just by using the app_.
            
        2.  It‚Äôs common to provide the ability to turn this off_,_ but only on higher-tier plans.
            
2.  Combine referrals with social.
    
    1.  Make the referral link extremely easy for customers to post to social. Two clicks max: One to express intent, one to post.
        
    2.  If you have user-generated content or brag-worthy usage statistics, have a low-friction way for users to share on social, and include the referral link.
        
3.  Combine referrals with community.
    
    1.  Create a public micro-site that identifies and thanks your top referrers. Reset this list every month to keep it fresh.
        
        1.  Notify them and let them do their magic in bragging about it.
            
        2.  Notify others to get them to compete for primacy.
            
    2.  Create a private community for your top referrers.
        
4.  Combine referrals with affiliate and influencer.
    
    1.  Use the same referral program and infrastructure with affiliates and influencers. Build once, use thrice!
        
    2.  Advertise these programs in your app to continuously recruit customers with large audiences. The ‚Äòrefer‚Äô screen is a perfect place for this.
        
5.  If you _also_ have an ‚Äòadd to team‚Äô flow to drive team expansion, make it work alongside your ‚Äòrefer‚Äô flow.
    
    1.  Start by deciding which to prioritize. Why? There will be situations where you can only pick one. Examples include limited pixels on screen or a finite ability to blast your customer base.
        
    2.  In my experience, a dollar of team expansion is worth more than a dollar of new logo. In this case:
        
        1.  Re-capture referrals as team adds where you think the user might want to grow their team, for example, if a user makes a same-domain referral like **adam@acme.com** referring **beth@acme.com**.
            
        2.  Prompt referees to join existing teams, where relevant. Yes, this voids any promised incentive, but the growing team probably benefits from the new member joining, and the referee avoids payment _forever_ by joining that team.
            
    3.  Revisit any previous assumptions made about your referral program. For example:
        
        1.  Deeply consider the copy. You might want to explicitly label them ‚ÄúRefer a friend‚Äù and ‚ÄúInvite a teammate‚Äù to clarify who each is for.
            
        2.  Update suggestion lists. You might want to _remove_ any contacts from the referral suggestion list that look like teammates.
            

### Weird Things ü•¥

*   Don‚Äôt cap referrals to create a sense of artificial scarcity.
    
    *   Referral curves are exponential. Your top referrer will do 100x the volume of your median referrer. Let whales be whales.
        
*   If you‚Äôre running a waitlist, you might consider asking _signups_ to refer to skip the wait.
    
    *   This only works if you have extreme marketing aura _and_ an extremely delightful product to justify the hack.
        
    *   Not impossible, but very delicate.
        
*   You might worry about incentive abuse and exploits.
    
    *   Put sensible safeguards in place, but don‚Äôt spend too much time on this. It‚Äôs easy to get bogged down in imaginary issues.
